\noindent\textbf{Startup context.} RailGuard Health is an EMR-embedded clinical workflow AI platform for outpatient clinics. The product automates documentation, coding/billing optimization, compliance checks, and follow-up tasks through SMART on FHIR integration.  
\textbf{Model scope.} This is a 5-year forecast for a venture-backed B2B SaaS business, expressed in CAD and structured to align with the assignment handout (Part 1: Cost-Revenue Model, Part 2: Financial Statements, Part 3: Financial Analysis).

\section*{Introduction}
RailGuard's thesis is that physician administrative overload is a persistent and monetizable pain point. The venture targets clinics with 1--10 clinicians, where buying cycles are shorter than hospitals and integration complexity is lower. The model assumes a Canada-first launch with US channel expansion beginning in Year 4.

Financially, the plan follows a typical healthcare SaaS pattern: heavy product and integration investment in Years 1--3, then operating leverage in Years 4--5 once onboarding playbooks, compliance workflows, and sales motion become repeatable. Revenue growth is driven by provider-seat expansion, modest ARPU lift from plan mix changes, and small implementation fees per clinic.

\subsection*{Risk and Sensitivity Discussion}
Three variables most threaten viability over five years:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Adoption velocity risk:} if provider adoption is 20\% below plan, break-even shifts from Y4 toward Y5.
\item \textbf{Pricing pressure risk:} if blended ARPU is 10\% lower (competitive discounting), EBITDA compression is significant in Y3--Y4.
\item \textbf{Integration/compliance risk:} delays in EMR integrations or privacy/compliance approvals can increase CAC and extend sales cycle.
\end{itemize}
On balance, the model appears fundable if early milestones are met: gross margin above 78\%, CAC payback below 9 months by Y2, and measurable pilot-to-paid conversion in target clinics.

\section*{Conclusion}
RailGuard's financial outlook is credible for an early-stage healthtech SaaS venture: front-loaded investment, increasing gross margin, and EBITDA positivity in Y4 under base-case assumptions. The model supports a staged financing strategy and offers an investor-readable path from pilot traction to scalable clinic penetration. The biggest execution requirement is disciplined conversion from integration work into repeatable, low-friction deployment and retention.
